addMarble(xPos, yPos, radius, color[optional])
  • xPos - The x-coordinate of where the marble spawns
  • yPos - The y-coordinate of where the marble spawns
  • radius - The radius of the marble
  • color[optional] - The hex code of the color you want (see example 2). Use google's color picker: https://g.co/kgs/Bt8tSX
Example 1:
addMarble(30, 20, 10)
 This would spawn a marble 30px to the right and 20px down (measured from the top-left corner of the screen) with a radius of 10px and a random color.

Example 2:
addMarble(30, 20, 10, "#FF10F0")
 This does exactly the same thing as Example 1 except the color will be #FF10F0 (neon pink) instead of random. Again, use google's color picker to figure out what the hex code of any color is: https://g.co/kgs/Bt8tSX

addTriangle(xPos, yPos, xRatio, yRatio, dilationFactor, color[optional])
  • xPos - The x-coordinate of the triangle's center of mass (note: the center of mass is the white dot in the middle of the triangle)
  • yPos - The y-coordinate of the triangle's center of mass
  • xRatio - The ratio of the triangle's width (use cos)
  • yRatio - The ratio of the triangle's height (use sin)
  • dilationFactor - Your x-ratio and y-ratio are always going to be values under 1 since you are using cos and sin. If you drew a triangle that was that width and that height, then it would be really small... so specify here how much you want to scale your triangle up. You'll probably want a value here between 10ish and 1000ish.
  • color[optional] - This works the same as it does in addMarble. See the full explanation there.
Example 1:
addTriangle(20, 200, cos(160), sin(160), 400)
This would generate a triangle with a center of mass (the white dot) 20px to the right and 200px down (measured from the top-left corner). The triangle drawn is the right triangle you would get if you drew a line at a 160 degree angle and then drew a line straight down to the x-axis at a right-angle. The dilation factor is 400 (so we are multiplying both the xRatio and yRatio by 400).

Example 2:
addTriangle(20, 200, cos(160), sin(160), 400, "#FFFF00")
This does exactly the same thing as Example 1 except the color will be #FFFF00 (yellow) instead of being blue by default. Again, use google's color picker to figure out what the hex code of any color is: https://g.co/kgs/Bt8tSX
